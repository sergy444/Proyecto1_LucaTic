UNMETAL ANALYSIS

The hooligan son of Hideo Kojima.

It is easy to get carried away by appearances and think that UnMetal intends to pay tribute, in a very personal way, to the Metal Gears of MSX, placing this particular tribute in an 8-bit frame, with its fixed cameras and pixel art. But the last game of Fran Téllez de Meneses goes further. A little more. Because honoring the pair of classics that Hideo Kojima sculpted for the legendary microcomputer is doing it with their polygon sequels and “Solid” tagline. Those that resurrected the franchise a decade later on PlayStation, made their way through the most inaccessible pantheons in video game history on PS2, reached their climax on PS3 and the epitome of their formula on PS4 and Xbox One. UnMetal is a fan gift to a saga. One that not just anyone can sign.

Hideo Kojimadid not invent the concept of infiltration in video games. It is imperative to talk about names like Shoplifting Boy and its sequel, Shoplifting Girl, Lupine III, Castle Wolfenstein and its continuation, Return to Castle Wolfenstein, Saboteur, or the Sega 005 game that, according to Guinness, is the first stealth title, pure and hard, which was done, back in 1985. But it was Kojima who solidified the infiltration into the absolute base of an action adventure, of unthinkable quality until then, contributing, and embedding, the elements of a genre that have not stopped embracing never since.

Without departing a millimeter from a premise, then almost revolutionary, today classic, UnMetal forces to avoid that its cartoon of Snake, Jessie Fox, is detected while it makes its way between troops, bosses, surveillance devices, closed doors and enemy obstacles. The protagonist's abilities are similar to those of Snake on his original journey, and he has a somewhat more modest arsenal and inventory. But just as effective. The combination of objects, of just levels of depth, the progression by levels, and the possibility of choosing between different types of enemies occasionally, make it possible for him to know how to provide the seal that distinguishes him from the works in which he is inspired without tarnishing them and make it unique.

HELL MODE IN RESIDENT EVIL REVELATIONS HD

Resident Evil Revelations HD will unleash all hell on Wii U, Xbox 360, PlayStation 3 and PC thanks to the introduction of a new high difficulty level. Capcom has released a new demo video where you can see some of the variations that will occur in this mode.

Activating Inferno Mode will not only mean increasing the number of enemies that will stalk us in each corner, their behavior will not be the same and their location will also be variable, as well as the placement of objects around the scenarios. This mode can be an interesting extra for those players who like challenges.

Resident Evil Revelations HD will arrive on May 24.

ALIEN: ISOLATION, A GREAT GAME THAT COULD BE THIRD PERSON

Well, yes, they are those kinds of things that surprise, when you have known and experienced something in a specific way and then they tell you that originally the idea was different and everything could have been different.

At first, Alien: Isolation was intended as a third-person perspective video game, as shown in this video presented by Creative Assembly during the Game Developers Convention.

We are so glad they finally settled on a subjective view. The words of Alistair Hope, creative director of the game, speak for themselves and close the possibility to any type of debate or discussion:

"In third person it looked like an Alien game, which is great, but in first person it looked like Alien."

Well, nothing, there is that.
